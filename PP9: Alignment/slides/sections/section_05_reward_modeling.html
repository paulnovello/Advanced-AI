Reward Modeling

Turning pairwise human preferences into a learnable scalar signal.

Architecture: Prompt + Response → Scalar Reward

Backbone: causal Transformer initialized from the SFT model — not random.
Scalar head: Linear(hidden_size, 1) — extracts reward from the EOS (last non-padding) hidden state.
Training: score both $\htmlClass{k-pref}{y_w}$ and $\htmlClass{k-pref}{y_l}$; apply BT loss. 1 epoch to avoid overfitting.

Bradley-Terry: Deriving the Preference Probability

\[ P\!\left(\htmlClass{k-pref}{i \succ j}\right) = \frac{\htmlClass{k-reward}{p_i}}{\htmlClass{k-reward}{p_i}+\htmlClass{k-reward}{p_j}} \]
Bradley-Terry (1952): each item has a latent strength $p_i > 0$.
\[ \htmlClass{k-reward}{p_i} = e^{\htmlClass{k-reward}{r_i}} \;\Rightarrow\; P\!\left(\htmlClass{k-pref}{i \succ j}\right) = \frac{e^{\htmlClass{k-reward}{r_i}}}{e^{\htmlClass{k-reward}{r_i}}+e^{\htmlClass{k-reward}{r_j}}} \]
Reparametrize with unbounded log-strength $r_i \in \mathbb{R}$. Only score differences matter.
\[ = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(\htmlClass{k-reward}{r_i}-\htmlClass{k-reward}{r_j})}} = \htmlClass{k-logp}{\sigma}\!\left(\htmlClass{k-reward}{r_i} - \htmlClass{k-reward}{r_j}\right) \]
Divide numerator and denominator by $e^{r_i}$; recognize the sigmoid $\sigma(z)=1/(1+e^{-z})$.
\[ P\!\left(\htmlClass{k-pref}{y_w \succ y_l} \mid x\right) = \htmlClass{k-logp}{\sigma}\!\left(\htmlClass{k-reward}{r_\theta(x, y_w)} - \htmlClass{k-reward}{r_\theta(x, y_l)}\right) \]
Substitute: $r_i \to \htmlClass{k-reward}{r_\theta(x,y_w)}$, $r_j \to \htmlClass{k-reward}{r_\theta(x,y_l)}$. Now the RM is a parametric model of preferences.
\[ P\!\left(\htmlClass{k-pref}{y_w \succ y_l} \mid x\right) = \htmlClass{k-logp}{\sigma}\!\Bigl(\htmlClass{k-reward}{r_\theta(x,\htmlClass{k-pref}{y_w})} - \htmlClass{k-reward}{r_\theta(x,\htmlClass{k-pref}{y_l})}\Bigr) \]
Adding a constant $c$ to every score leaves all probabilities unchanged — scores are defined up to a shift. The sigmoid only sees the difference $\Delta r$.

From Preference Probability to the Training Loss

\[ \theta^* = \arg\max_\theta\; P\!\left(\htmlClass{k-pref}{y_w \succ y_l} \mid x\right) = \arg\max_\theta\; \htmlClass{k-logp}{\sigma}\!\left(\htmlClass{k-reward}{r_\theta(x,y_w)} - \htmlClass{k-reward}{r_\theta(x,y_l)}\right) \]
Start from the preference probability derived on the previous slide; maximize the likelihood of the observed preference.
\[ = \arg\min_\theta\; \htmlClass{k-logp}{-\log\,\sigma}\!\left(\htmlClass{k-reward}{r_\theta(x,y_w)} - \htmlClass{k-reward}{r_\theta(x,y_l)}\right) \]
$\arg\max f = \arg\min (-\log f)$; log is monotone, so the optimum is unchanged. This is the NLL.
\[ \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \htmlClass{k-logp}{-\,\mathbb{E}_{(x,y_w,y_l)\sim\mathcal{D}}} \Bigl[\log\,\htmlClass{k-logp}{\sigma}\!\left( \htmlClass{k-reward}{r_\theta(x,y_w)} - \htmlClass{k-reward}{r_\theta(x,y_l)} \right)\Bigr] \]
Average NLL over the preference dataset $\mathcal{D}$. This is the Bradley-Terry RM loss.
In code (numerically stable): -F.logsigmoid(r_chosen - r_rejected).mean().
Variants exist — margin term $m(y_w,y_l)$ (Llama 2), K-wise Plackett–Luce ranking (Starling) — but plain Bradley–Terry remains the default.

Reward Model Variants: ORM vs PRM

The BT model scores the whole sequence once. Two variants add finer-grained supervision for reasoning tasks.

ORM: single binary label $r \in \{0,1\}$ on the entire completion. Per-token BCE loss. Good for verifiable domains (math, code). Does not catch reasoning errors mid-sequence.
PRM: label $y_{s_i} \in \{-1,0,+1\}$ at each step boundary (e.g., newline token). Per-step CE loss. Enables pruning incorrect reasoning branches during search.

System prompt (MT-Bench, Zheng et al. 2023):
"Please act as an impartial judge… output [[A]] if assistant A is better, [[B]] if B is better, [[C]] for a tie."
  • Spawned automated evaluation benchmarks: AlpacaEval, Arena-Hard, WildBench.
  • Models trained specifically to be judges ("Generative RMs") are an active research area — but still lag dedicated scalar RMs on RM benchmarks (RewardBench, RMB, RewardBench2).
  • Practical tip: use temperature = 0 to reduce rating variance.

The RM is a Proxy — It Can Diverge

The reward model approximates human preference — it is not human preference itself. Policy optimization exploits gaps in the learned surface.

Reward hacking: the policy finds completions that score high on the RM but that humans would not actually prefer — the RM's preference surface has gaps and artefacts.
Over-optimization: the longer you train against the RM proxy, the worse it gets. The RM score peaks and then collapses relative to true human preference.
Mitigation — KL regularization: add $\htmlClass{k-kl}{\beta \cdot D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_{\mathrm{ref}})}$ to the objective. Stops the policy from wandering too far into RM blind spots. → Next section: PPO/GRPO and the full RL objective.
