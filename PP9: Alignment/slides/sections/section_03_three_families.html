Three Families of Optimization Tools

All three solve the same underlying objective — they differ in how they query the reward and when they update the policy.

The Three Families — Data-Flow View

(a) Policy-Gradient RL
πθ generates live completions → RM scores → gradient update. Needs live rollouts + explicit RM.
PPO / GRPO
(b) Direct Alignment (DPO)
Re-parameterises reward implicitly via log-ratio $\pi_\theta/\pi_\text{ref}$. Fixed preference pairs supervise πθ — no separate RM.
DPO / IPO / KTO
(c) Rejection Sampling
Sample N, keep top scored by RM, fine-tune on winners. No gradient through reward — simplest RL-like loop.
RS / Best-of-N
All three optimise the same KL-regularised objective — they differ in when reward is queried and whether updates are on-policy.

Why RL still wins where it matters: ① optimizes non-differentiable rewards (code compiles, math verifies); ② trains on on-policy data — targets the model's own failure modes; ③ can exceed the SFT ceiling — R1-Zero discovered CoT reasoning no demonstration contained.
Rule of thumb: start with DPO for simplicity; upgrade to RL when you need on-policy exploration or verifiable rewards (math, code); use rejection sampling to bootstrap SFT data cheaply.
